Editing Is Not Writing, and Mixing Them Up Slows Everything Down

April 27, 2026 · 5 min read

For a long time I edited as I went: write a sentence, reread it, adjust a word, move on. It felt careful and responsible, like proofreading a form before submitting it. It also meant a single paragraph could eat twenty minutes, and a full draft could take days longer than it needed to.

Two different jobs, one keyboard

Writing a new sentence and judging an existing one use different mental postures. Writing wants momentum and a willingness to be wrong. Editing wants suspicion and a willingness to cut. Switching between them constantly, sentence by sentence, means neither one gets to run properly — the writing part keeps getting interrupted by the editing part's doubts, before it's even had a chance to get anywhere.

Writing wants momentum. Editing wants suspicion. You can't do both at once without both losing.

What changed when I separated them

The current rule is simple: during a first draft, the delete key doesn't get touched. Bad sentence, wrong word, whole paragraph going nowhere — none of it gets fixed in the moment. It gets a bracket, like [fix this], and the draft keeps moving forward. Editing happens afterward, in its own separate pass, on a printed page or a completely different screen, days later if possible.

The draft that comes out of this is messier than the sentence-by-sentence version used to be. But it exists in a fraction of the time, and a messy finished draft is infinitely more useful than a clean unfinished one, because only one of them can actually be edited.

The part that took getting used to

The hardest part wasn't the method, it was tolerating a paragraph I knew was bad and moving past it anyway. That discomfort never fully goes away. It just gets outweighed, every time, by how much faster the draft gets finished — and a finished bad paragraph can be fixed. An unwritten good one can't.
