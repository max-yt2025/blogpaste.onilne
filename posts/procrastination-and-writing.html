Procrastination Was Never the Real Problem

May 12, 2026 · 4 min read

For years I described myself as a procrastinator, as if that were a fixed trait, like handedness. I'd put off writing for days, feel guilty about it, and eventually produce something at the last possible minute under real pressure. The lesson I took from that pattern was "I work better under deadline," which felt like a compliment I was giving myself.

It wasn't a compliment. It was a workaround for a different problem I hadn't named yet.

What the deadline actually removed

Looking back, the deadline wasn't adding motivation so much as removing an option: the option to demand the piece be good on the first attempt. With no time left, "good" stopped being available, so "finished" became the only remaining goal. That's the actual ingredient that was doing the work — not urgency, but permission to write something bad quickly.

The deadline wasn't adding pressure. It was removing the demand that the first draft be good.

Giving myself the permission earlier

Once I noticed that, the fix wasn't a stricter schedule or a habit tracker. It was giving myself, on purpose and in advance, the exact permission the deadline used to force on me: the first version is allowed to be bad. Saying that explicitly, before starting, removed most of the delay that used to build up in the days before a deadline.

I still procrastinate sometimes. But it's much rarer now, and when it happens it's usually a sign that I'm quietly expecting too much from a first attempt again, not a character flaw that needs managing.

A smaller definition of "later"

The other change was shrinking what "later" was allowed to mean. "I'll write it later" used to mean sometime this week. Now the rule is that if I'm not starting in the next ten minutes, I write one sentence right now instead — badly, incompletely, it doesn't matter. That one sentence usually turns out to be enough to make "later" unnecessary.
