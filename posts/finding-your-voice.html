Finding Your Voice Is Mostly Subtraction

May 21, 2026 · 5 min read

"Find your voice" is one of those pieces of writing advice that sounds true and explains nothing. It implies a voice is sitting somewhere inside you, fully formed, waiting to be discovered like a key under a doormat. In my experience it's closer to the opposite: you don't find a voice underneath everything, you dig one out from under everything.

Most early writing is impersonation

Nearly everything I wrote in my twenties sounded like whoever I'd read most recently. A few weeks of a particular essayist and my sentences would start doing that writer's tricks — their rhythm, their favorite sentence openers, their fondness for short paragraphs after a long one. None of it was mine. All of it was on loan.

That's not a character flaw, it's just how style gets learned, the same way a kid picks up a parent's expressions before knowing they're borrowed. The problem only shows up if you never notice you're doing it and never take any of it back off.

Subtraction, not addition

The exercise that actually helped wasn't reading more different writers to blend together. It was going back through my own drafts and asking, sentence by sentence: would I say this out loud, to a person, in these exact words? Anything that failed that test — usually the fanciest, most "writerly" lines — got cut.

What was left after cutting the impressive-sounding parts turned out to be the voice I was looking for the whole time.

What was left afterward was plainer than I expected and, embarrassingly, closer to how I actually talk. That plainness turned out to be the thing readers responded to, not the borrowed flourishes I'd been so careful to keep in.

A voice is a leftover, not a discovery

If "finding your voice" isn't working as an idea, try the opposite framing: assume your voice is already there, underneath the parts copied from other people, and your job is just to remove enough of the copying to see it.
