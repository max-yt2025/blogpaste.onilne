The Perfect Notebook Doesn't Exist, and Looking for It Was the Problem

March 30, 2026 · 4 min read

At one point I owned four different notebooks, each purchased because the previous one had a flaw — paper too thin, cover too soft, pages that didn't lie flat. I told myself I was optimizing my tools. What I was actually doing was avoiding the much less pleasant task of just writing something in whichever one I already had.

The search was the avoidance

Shopping for the right notebook feels productive in a way that's almost indistinguishable from working, especially at first. Comparing paper weights and binding styles occupies the same part of your attention that planning a piece of writing does, and it's a lot more comfortable, because a notebook can't disappoint you the way an actual sentence can.

Comparing notebooks felt like working, and cost none of the discomfort that actual writing does.

What finally ended it

Nothing dramatic — I just ran out of the current notebook on a day with no replacement in the house, grabbed a cheap one from a drawer, and kept going. The cheap one worked exactly as well as any of the expensive ones had. That was the moment the whole search collapsed under its own weight; there had never actually been a meaningful difference, only the feeling of one.

What I use now

Whatever notebook is nearest when I need one. No brand loyalty, no paper-weight requirements, no research before buying. The one rule that survived the whole experiment: buy two at a time, so running out is never again an excuse to go shopping instead of writing.
