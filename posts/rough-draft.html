The Art of the Rough Draft

June 24, 2026 · 5 min read

Somewhere along the way, "rough draft" started sounding like an apology. As if the real writing happens later, in some polished final form, and the rough draft is just the embarrassing thing you have to get through first.

I think that framing is backwards. The rough draft isn't a lesser version of the finished piece. It's the only tool anyone has ever used to find out what the finished piece is supposed to say.

You can't edit a blank page

This is the part everyone already knows and nobody quite believes. Editing is a real skill — cutting, reordering, tightening — but it needs raw material to work on. A rough draft's entire job is to exist, badly if necessary, so that editing has something to do.

The paralysis most people call "writer's block" is usually just an unwillingness to write something bad on the way to writing something good. Once you stop expecting the first pass to be right, it stops being scary. It's not supposed to be right. It's supposed to be there.

A rough draft's only job is to exist so editing has something to cut.

How I actually do it

A few things that made rough drafts easier for me, in no particular order:

  • Write the ending first if the beginning won't come. Drafts don't have to be written in order.
  • Leave visible gaps — [insert the statistic here] — instead of stopping to look something up mid-sentence.
  • Set a low, almost insulting word count goal, so finishing feels easy instead of heroic.
  • Don't reread while writing. Rereading is editing wearing a disguise, and it slows the draft down for no benefit.

The paste, not the polish

This blog is named after the part of writing that happens before anything is polished: getting the words down somewhere, however rough, so there's something to work with later. Most of what ends up here started as a much worse version of itself. That's not a flaw in the process. It's the whole process.
