Why I Started Pasting My Thoughts Here

July 1, 2026 · 4 min read

For about six years I kept notebooks. Cheap ones, mostly, bought a few at a time so I'd never run out. I filled them with half-formed arguments, grocery lists, drafts of emails I was too nervous to send, and the occasional attempt at something like an essay. When a notebook was full, I put it on a shelf and started a new one.

I never read them again. Not once. The shelf was less an archive than a graveyard, and I knew it, and I kept writing anyway, because the writing itself felt useful even when nothing came of it afterward.

What finally changed wasn't a productivity system or a New Year's resolution. It was smaller and dumber than that: I got tired of the fact that finishing a thought and then losing it felt like the default outcome of thinking at all.

The difference an audience makes

I'm not claiming anyone is waiting for these posts. Most days, nobody reads them, and that's fine. But writing for a page that could in theory be read by someone else does something that a private notebook never managed to do for me: it makes half-finished thoughts uncomfortable to leave half-finished.

A private notebook lets you stop mid-sentence forever. A public post, even a small and unglamorous one, nags at you to actually land somewhere. That nagging turned out to be the whole trick.

The goal was never an audience. The goal was a reason to finish the sentence.

What this blog actually is

It's not a newsletter with a content calendar, and it's not trying to build anything. It's closer to a corkboard than a magazine: short pieces, pinned up when they're ready, about writing, notes, and the small habits around both. Some weeks there will be two posts. Some months there will be none. That's the deal I've made with myself, and so far it's the only version of "writing in public" that hasn't quietly died within a season.

If you're the kind of person who has a shelf of unread notebooks too, this whole site is basically an argument for pasting the good bits somewhere you might actually look at them again.
