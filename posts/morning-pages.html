What Morning Pages Actually Do

May 30, 2026 · 5 min read

Morning pages are a simple rule: three handwritten pages, first thing, before you look at a phone or open email. You don't reread them. You don't edit them. You just fill three pages with whatever is in your head, even if what's in your head is "I don't know what to write."

I was skeptical of this for a long time because it sounded like a wellness trend wearing a writing costume. A year in, I don't think that anymore, though it's also not what I expected.

It's not for producing anything

This is the part that took the longest to accept. Morning pages are not a source of material. I've gone back through old ones looking for a salvageable line and found almost nothing — mostly complaints about weather, half-remembered dreams, and the same three worries on rotation.

That's not a failure of the exercise. The pages aren't there to produce a draft. They're there to empty out the noise that would otherwise sit at the front of your head all morning, competing with whatever you actually meant to write.

The pages aren't a draft. They're what you clear out of the way before the draft.

What actually changed

The concrete difference, after doing this daily for a year: the writing I do afterward starts faster. There used to be a warm-up period, ten or fifteen minutes of throat-clearing sentences before anything usable showed up. That warm-up now happens on the page nobody reads, instead of the page that's supposed to count.

Where it falls apart

On genuinely busy mornings, three full pages by hand is a real time cost, and I've skipped weeks at a stretch when travel or deadlines got in the way. I used to feel guilty about that. I don't anymore — the pages are a tool for clearing your head, not a moral obligation, and a tool you feel guilty about using is one you'll eventually stop using altogether.
