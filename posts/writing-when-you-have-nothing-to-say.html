Writing When You Have Nothing to Say

March 20, 2026 · 4 min read

Most mornings I sit down with nothing worth writing. Not a dramatic block, just an ordinary, unremarkable emptiness — no idea, no angle, no sentence waiting to be finished. For a long time I treated that as a signal to wait, on the theory that writing without an idea is a waste of time. It isn't. Waiting for the idea first was the actual waste.

The idea usually shows up during, not before

Almost every post on this site started with a sentence that had no idea behind it — a description of a feeling, a complaint, an observation with no clear point. The idea arrived a paragraph or two later, once there was already something on the page to react to. Waiting for it to arrive first, with a blank page, almost never works, because there's nothing yet for the idea to attach itself to.

The idea usually shows up a paragraph after you needed it, not before you started.

What "nothing to say" actually means

In practice, "I have nothing to say" almost always means "I have nothing finished to say," which is a completely different and much less alarming problem. Nobody starts a piece of writing with a finished idea. They start with a fragment, and the fragment gets finished by writing toward it, not by thinking about it in advance until it's ready.

The actual practice

On the emptiest days, the rule is to write one true, boring sentence about whatever's actually happening — tired, distracted, staring at a blank page — and then write the next sentence in response to that one. It's a low bar on purpose. Most of those sessions go nowhere. A few of them, unpredictably, turn into the post you're reading right now.
